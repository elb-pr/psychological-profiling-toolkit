## Evidence Synthesis

### Finding 1: Core Taxonomy
Beck (1963, 1964): Systematic errors in thinking identified from clinical observation — selective abstraction, arbitrary inference, over-generalisation, magnification/minimisation, personalisation. Burns (1980): Extended taxonomy to ten cognitive distortions, adding all-or-nothing thinking, jumping to conclusions (mind-reading + fortune-telling), labelling, should-statements, emotional reasoning. Burns's 1980 taxonomy is the most operationalised version — each distortion has specific linguistic surface features. CLASSIFICATION: ESTABLISHED as clinical taxonomy; ESTABLISHED as phenomenological categories; EMERGING as precisely distinct empirical constructs.

### Finding 2: Linguistic Markers — Direct Documentary Application
European Proceedings (2021): Linguistic analysis of automatic thoughts and cognitive distortions in naturalistic text. Key finding: each distortion has grammatically identifiable markers. All-or-nothing: absolute quantifiers (always/never/everyone/no one). Over-generalisation: broad scope claims from limited evidence ("People always..."). Should-statements: modal imperatives. Mind-reading: third-person internal state attribution without evidence. Fortune-telling: future-negative certainty without hedging. Magnification: superlative/extreme language. Personalisation: causal self-attribution for external events. CLASSIFICATION: EMERGING (single study, N=47).

### Finding 3: Automatic Thoughts Questionnaire (ATQ) — Observable Thought Content
Hollon & Kendall (1980) ATQ: 30 negative automatic thoughts, self-reported frequency. Content maps directly onto documentary text analysis — ATQ items are verbatim thought expressions that appear as documentary signals. Burns (1980) Dysfunctional Attitudes Scale provides schema-level markers. CLASSIFICATION: ESTABLISHED for self-report; EMERGING for documentary inference.

### Finding 4: NLP and Automated Detection
Burns' patterns text (PDF): explicit linguistic examples per distortion, directly usable as coding dictionary. Psychology Tools (2019): operationalised unhelpful thinking styles with linguistic examples for CBT — clinically validated coding guide. CLASSIFICATION: ESTABLISHED as practitioner coding guide (Tier 5).

### Contradictions
| Contradiction | Type | Position A | Position B |
|---|---|---|---|
| Distinct constructs vs general negativity | Definitional | Burns: ten distinct distortions | Factor analysis studies: 2–3 factors underlie all ten |
| Distortion vs valid negative cognition | Paradigmatic | Beck: negative cognitions are systematically distorted | Realistic thinking researchers: some "depressive realism" is accurate |

### Evidence Gaps
1. No inter-rater reliability study for trained human coders applying Burns taxonomy to documentary text (vs CBT transcripts)
2. Depressive realism boundary: when does realistic negative cognition become pathological distortion? Not operationally defined for documentary inference

## Transferability Verdict
**VIABLE** — Burns (1980) taxonomy maps distortion categories directly onto observable linguistic surface forms. The framework requires no direct subject access because the distortions ARE language patterns. Confidence varies by distortion: all-or-nothing, over-generalisation, should-statements HIGH confidence; mind-reading, emotional reasoning LOWER confidence (attribution-dependent).

## Documentary Proxy Map
| Distortion | Primary Linguistic Signal | Confidence |
|---|---|---|
| All-or-nothing thinking | Always/never/everyone/no one; categorical absolute framing | HIGH |
| Over-generalisation | Broad claims from single instance; "typical" / "as usual" | HIGH |
| Should-statements | Modal imperatives: should/must/ought/have to | HIGH |
| Magnification | Superlatives; catastrophe language; extreme descriptors | HIGH |
| Personalisation | Causal self-attribution for external/unrelated events | MODERATE |
| Mind-reading | Internal state attribution to others without evidence | MODERATE |
| Fortune-telling | Negative future certainty without hedging | MODERATE |
| Emotional reasoning | "I feel X therefore X is true" structure | LOW — requires emotional self-disclosure |
