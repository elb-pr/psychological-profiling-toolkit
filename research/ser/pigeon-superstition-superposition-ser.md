## Evidence Synthesis

### Finding 1: Skinner Superstition Paradigm
Skinner (1948): food-deprived pigeons develop idiosyncratic repetitive behaviours
under fixed-interval non-contingent reinforcement. Interpreted as operant conditioning
of adventitiously reinforced responses. Full text open access [web:337].
Superstitious key-pecking study (1970): superstitious behaviour arises within 3
peck-produced reinforcements, confirming low threshold for illusory causation [web:333].
CLASSIFICATION: ESTABLISHED for animal behaviour; THEORETICAL for human documentary analogy

### Finding 2: Staddon & Simmelhag Reanalysis
Staddon & Simmelhag (1971): reanalysis distinguishes terminal (food-directed) from
interim (schedule-filling) behaviours. "Superstition" is not random conditioning but
species-typical appetitive behaviour triggered by temporal proximity to reward.
Implication for profiling: subject's default behaviours under uncertainty may be
species-typical (appetitive) OR idiosyncratically conditioned — the distinction matters
for inference. CLASSIFICATION: ESTABLISHED (reanalysis); changes interpretation of Skinner

### Finding 3: Bradford Hill Criteria as Scoring Framework
Hill (1965): nine criteria for causal inference from observational data — strength,
consistency, specificity, temporality, biological gradient, plausibility, coherence,
experiment, analogy [web:335][web:336].
PMC (2014): half-century review confirms criteria remain the standard framework for
causal inference in epidemiology and beyond [web:335].
European Journal (2015): Bradford Hill in 21st century — criteria survive data integration
and molecular era [web:331]. Causal assessment in evidence synthesis review (2022):
Bradford Hill most widely applied framework across disciplines [web:334].
CLASSIFICATION: ESTABLISHED as causal inference framework

### Finding 4: Signal Detection Theory
Green & Swets (1966): d′ (sensitivity) and β (criterion) separate signal detection
ability from response bias. Directly applicable to documentary inference:
- d′ = how reliably a causal claim can be distinguished from noise in the evidence
- β = how conservatively the analyst sets threshold for claiming causation
Applied to superstitious attribution: subject with low d′ assigns causation where only
correlation exists; subject with low β (liberal criterion) overclaims causal connections.
CLASSIFICATION: ESTABLISHED in psychophysics; THEORETICAL for documentary personality inference

### Finding 5: Contradictions
- Skinner's interpretation (adventitious conditioning) vs Staddon's (species-typical)
  remains unresolved for human analogy. Nature: EMPIRICAL/PARADIGMATIC.
- Bradford Hill criteria not universally accepted as sufficient for causal claims —
  Bradford Hill himself stated they were neither necessary nor sufficient. Nature: EMPIRICAL.

### Evidence Gaps
1. No published study applying Skinner/Staddon framework to human documentary inference.
2. No validated inter-rater reliability protocol for scoring superstitious attribution
   from documentary sources using Bradford Hill dimensions.

## Transferability Verdict
VIABLE — Bradford Hill criteria and signal detection framework are explicitly designed
for observational (non-experimental) causal inference from existing evidence. The Skinner
paradigm functions as theoretical framework; Bradford Hill provides the operational scoring.
Transfer gap: MINOR for causal claim assessment; THEORETICAL for full wavefunction scoring.
