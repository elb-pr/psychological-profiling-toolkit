## Evidence Synthesis

### Finding 1: Actuarial vs Clinical Prediction Debate
Meehl (1954): actuarial methods outperform clinical judgement in 19 of 20 comparative
studies. Grove et al. (2000) meta-analysis (N=136 studies): actuarial methods equal or
superior to clinical judgement in 90% of comparisons.
CLASSIFICATION: ESTABLISHED — one of the most replicated findings in applied psychology

### Finding 2: HCR-20 — Documentary Risk Assessment Instrument (Critical)
Douglas, Webster, Hart, Eaves & Ogloff (2001): HCR-20 (Historical-Clinical-Risk Management)
structured professional judgement (SPJ) instrument. Historical section (H items) operates
entirely from documentary record — previous violence, early maladjustment, relationship
instability, employment problems, substance use, major mental illness, psychopathy score,
early violent attitudes, supervision failure.
HCR-20 is explicitly designed for situations where direct interview is unavailable or
supplemented by documentary review. Validated predictor of institutional and community
violence. AUC=.76–.83 across multiple studies.
CLASSIFICATION: ESTABLISHED — Tier 3 validated forensic instrument

### Finding 3: Structured Professional Judgement Framework
Kropp & Hart (2000): SPJ framework combines actuarial base rates with structured
clinical judgement — explicit anchoring to empirically validated risk factors with
professional override capacity. This is the operational model for the profiling system.
Douglas & Kropp (2002): prevention-based paradigm — risk assessment from historical
and contextual factors in the documentary record produces actionable risk characterisation.
CLASSIFICATION: ESTABLISHED

### Finding 4: Psychopathy as Documentary Construct
Hare (1991): PCL-R scored from file review and interview. File review alone (PCL:SV
file review version) maintains adequate predictive validity (AUC=.71–.78) without
direct clinical interview. Demonstrates that documentary evidence alone supports
clinically meaningful psychopathy scoring.
CLASSIFICATION: ESTABLISHED for PCL-R file review; note PCL-R requires trained forensic clinician

### Finding 5: Multi-Source Synthesis Validity
Monahan et al. (2001): MacArthur risk study demonstrates multiple actuarial domains
(psychiatric, historical, contextual, dispositional) combined improve prediction above
single-domain approaches. Multi-source documentary synthesis is methodologically sound.
CLASSIFICATION: ESTABLISHED

### Finding 6: Contradictions
- Actuarial superiority challenged for novel/unprecedented cases: base rates from
  previous populations may not apply. Nature: EMPIRICAL.
- SPJ vs pure actuarial: professional override can improve or degrade accuracy
  depending on clinician expertise. Nature: EMPIRICAL.
- HCR-20 validated primarily for violence prediction; application to non-forensic
  profiling requires careful construct mapping. Nature: DEFINITIONAL.

### Evidence Gaps
1. No published validation of HCR-20 adapted for non-forensic (intelligence/
   investigative) documentary profiling.
2. Base rate calibration for non-clinical general populations using forensic risk
   instruments is not established — overclaiming risk is a structural failure mode.

## Transferability Verdict
VIABLE — HCR-20 Historical section is explicitly designed for documentary inference.
PCL-R file review version confirms psychopathy scoring from records alone is valid.
Actuarial frameworks are structurally independent of direct interview.
Transfer gap: MINOR for H-items of HCR-20; MODERATE for C and R items (clinical and
risk management items require current state assessment).

## Failure Mode Register
| Failure Mode | Likelihood |
|---|---|
| Applying forensic base rates to non-forensic subject population | LIKELY |
| Over-claiming certainty from SPJ — SPJ is a guide to judgement, not a verdict | LIKELY |
| H-item inference from incomplete documentary record (survivorship bias) | LIKELY |
| Conflating violence risk constructs with general behavioural risk | POSSIBLE |
